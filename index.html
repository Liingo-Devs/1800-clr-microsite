01. A little context

Why the Fairness to Contact Lens Consumers Act (FCLCA) exists

Eye doctors make up to half of their income off selling the very thing they prescribe: contacts and glasses. This conflict of interest has led to some egregious behavior over the years from some eye doctors who want to limit their patients’ choice so they have buy contacts and glasses from them.

In 2004 the Fairness to Contact Lens Consumer Act was enacted which requires consumers to receive a copy of their prescription for free, without having to ask for it. Seems fair.

02. More bad behavior

Eye doctors continue to break the law

Unfortunately, over the last 16 years eye doctors continue to withhold patients’ prescriptions, charge extra for them, or use scare tactics (“You have special eyes and the only place you can get your contacts is from me”) to preserve their margins at their customers’ expense. Literally.

03. FCLCA protects consumers

The Contact Lens Rule: protecting consumers and holding eye doctors accountable

With many of the same issues still facing consumers that the FCLCA was supposed to protect against, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) unanimously approved the Contact Lens Rule which requires eye doctors to get and keep a signed acknowledgment that the patient received their prescription.

Many eye doctors don’t want to be held accountable to the law and are making excuses about why they shouldn’t be.
